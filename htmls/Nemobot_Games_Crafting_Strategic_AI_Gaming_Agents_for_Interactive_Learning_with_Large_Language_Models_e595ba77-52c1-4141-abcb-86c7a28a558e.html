Section 2 - Abstract

📄 原文

This paper introduces a new paradigm for AI game programming, leveraging large language models (LLMs) to extend and operationalize Claude Shannon’s taxonomy of game-playing machines. Central to this paradigm is Nemobot, an interactive agentic engineering environment that enables users to create, customize, and deploy LLM-powered game agents while actively engaging with AI-driven strategies. The LLM-based chatbot, integrated within Nemobot, demonstrates its capabilities across four distinct classes of games. For dictionary-based games, it compresses state-action mappings into efficient, generalized models for rapid adaptability. In rigorously solvable games, it employs mathematical reasoning to compute optimal strategies and generates human-readable explanations for its decisions. For heuristic-based games, it synthesizes strategies by combining insights from classical minimax algorithms (see, e.g., [1]) with crowd-sourced data. Finally, in learning-based games, it utilizes reinforcement learning with human feedback and self-critique to iteratively refine strategies through trial-and-error and imitation learning. Nemobot amplifies this framework by offering a programmable environment where users can experiment with tool-augmented generation and fine-tuning of strategic game agents. From strategic games to role-playing games, Nemobot demonstrates how AI agents can achieve a form of self-programming by integrating crowdsourced learning and human creativity to iteratively refine their own logic. This represents a step toward the long-term goal of self-programming AI.

摘要

如果說電腦程式是教機器「怎麼做」，那麼有沒有可能讓機器自己學會「怎麼想」？這篇論文回答了這個問題——它把人工智慧放進遊戲世界裡，讓AI不只會玩遊戲，還能像人類一樣邊玩邊學、邊學邊變強。

一切始於一位叫 Claude Shannon 的資訊學祖師爺。1950年，他幫遊戲程式分了類：哪些遊戲有標準答案、哪些只能靠「感覺」判斷、哪些要從錯誤中學習。這套分類法過了七十多年，終於被新一代的大型語言模型接棒了。

研究者打造了一個叫做 Nemobot 的互動系統，你可以把它想像成「AI遊戲實驗室」。在這個環境裡，使用者可以自己動手做出各種會玩遊戲的AI代理程式，而且不用寫死一堆if語句——AI會自己推理、自己優化。

這個系統有多厲害？它能應付四種完全不同性格的遊戲：

第一類是字典遊戲（如圍棋、橋牌）。這類遊戲的招式雖然多，但有跡可循。Nemobot能把幾百萬種可能的走法「壓縮」成一套聰明的原則，讓它學一次就能舉一反三。

第二類是嚴格可解的遊戲（如西洋棋、井字遊戲）。這種遊戲有標準答案——只是答案太多了，窮舉不完。Nemobot會用數學推理找出最優策略，而且厲害的是，它還能「用人話解釋」自己為什麼這樣下棋。

第三類是啟發式遊戲（如麻將、部分撲克局）。這裡沒有完美解答，需要在模糊資訊中做判斷。Nemobot結合了傳統的minimax演算法與網路上群眾的智慧，既能精算、又能接地氣。

第四類是學習型遊戲（如新型態的策略遊戲）。這是AI最像人類的地方——它會自己和自己對戰，從錯誤中吸取教訓，並根據人類的回饋調整方向。

最後，Nemobot把這些能力全部整合成一個可程式化的環境。這意味著你不只是旁觀者，而是設計者——可以幫AI加上各種工具、訓練它更聰明的思考方式，甚至讓它自己改進自己的邏輯。

這篇論文展示的，不只是「AI會玩遊戲」，而是一個更深的可能性：當AI學會自己編寫自己的時候，會發生什麼？ 這也許是我們走向「自我程式化AI」的第一步。

Section 3 - I Introduction

📄 原文

Claude Shannon, a pioneer in artificial intelligence, posed a thought-provoking question in his seminal work [2]: “Can we program a digital computer so that (eventually) 99 percent of the orders it follows are written by the computer itself, rather than the few percent in current programs?” This question encapsulates the vision of self-programming systems, a concept that remains a core challenge in AI research. Similarly, Marvin Minsky argued in [3] that programming’s structured rules are not limitations but tools for refining and clarifying poorly understood ideas, akin to how artists and composers use formal constraints to express creativity. Shannon’s vision of computers autonomously generating their own instructions has become increasingly relevant in agentic engineering, where large language models (LLMs) now produce vast amounts of code, driving self-evolving AI systems and interactive applications such as games.

Figure 1: Crowdsourcing and strategy optimization in game-playing AI. The LLM generates optimized strategies for game-playing agents, while game states and results from interactions with human players are fed back to train the LLM, creating a self-reinforcing cycle of improvement through crowdsourcing. Figure 2: Shannon’s four types of game-playing machines and their typical examples of applications.

Recent surveys, such as [4, 5], explore advances in strategic AI game agents to enhance adaptability and reasoning using LLMs. Research into specific applications such as combining language models with strategic reasoning in the game of Diplomacy [6] and using LLMs as testers to measure game difficulty [7] demonstrates the versatility of LLMs in imperfect-information games. Approaches like ChessGPT [8] and AlphaStar [9] bridge policy learning with language modeling, emphasizing the importance of synergizing reinforcement learning and natural language processing. The development of systems such as Suspicion-Agent [10], which integrates theory-of-mind-aware reasoning with OpenAI’s GPT-4, further extends the horizon of multi-agent interactions in imperfect information settings.

Game boards serve as microworlds: discrete, observable world states that provide a structured, controllable testbed for studying representation learning of world models in LLMs. When trained on game transcripts and rules, LLMs can learn implicit representations that encode not only legal moves but also temporal dependencies, hidden states, and probabilistic opponent strategies. For instance, [11] demonstrate that chess gameplay enables LLMs to track board states and predict moves, providing evidence that strategic environments can induce meaningful internal representations of game dynamics. This aligns with findings by [12], who show that transformers trained on synthetic sequential prediction tasks spontaneously form internal world models capturing latent environmental dynamics without explicit supervision. To rigorously evaluate the fidelity of these learned representations, [13] propose systematic methods for probing whether generative models maintain consistent beliefs about unobserved variables and adhere to transition rules. Collectively, these studies suggest that gameplay can drive representational learning of world models in LLMs though these models often remain approximate and lack explicit grounding.

To refine and leverage these representations, AI game programming provides a powerful paradigm for structured world-model learning. By designing game simulators and programmed interfaces, we can create environments where an LLM agent must learn explicit, verifiable representations to succeed. For example, an agentic engineering framework can be designed to interact with a game engine through a structured API, forcing the LLM to explicitly represent board states, action spaces, and opponent models to generate valid function calls. This approach uses programmable feedback loops to ground abstract internal representations: the LLM’s predictions are immediately validated or corrected by the game engine, enabling error-driven refinement of its world model. Through repeated interaction with these programmed interfaces, an AI agent can learn more consistent, accurate, and generalizable representations, effectively turning gameplay into a form of self-supervised representation learning.

These programmable approaches result in world models that are not only more interpretable but also more actionable for downstream strategic reasoning, thereby narrowing the gap between emergent internal representations and externally verifiable game semantics. This downstream strategic reasoning can be further enhanced through established methods for improving language model reasoning: self-consistency [14] refines decision quality via structured reasoning chains, while frameworks like ReAct [15] and Graph of Thoughts [16] provide formal mechanisms for reasoning and planning in complex environments. Moreover, work on retrieval-augmented strategic play [17] demonstrates how language models can dynamically incorporate external knowledge to guide exploration and improve gameplay by using LLM-based agents.

Recent advances in AI-assisted programming are transforming the development of sophisticated game AI agents. Foundation models like OpenAI’s GPT harness advanced NLP and LLM capabilities to interpret human instructions and generate functional code [18]. This has enabled tools such as GitHub Copilot [19], Copilot for Xcode [20, 21], OpenAI Codex [22], and Claude Code [23] to support programmers in rapidly prototyping, debugging, and optimizing agent logic, thus accelerating the design of reasoning loops, tool integration and strategic modules for game AI. The evolving multimodal capabilities of publicly-available LLM systems like OpenAI’s GPT [24] and Google’s Gemini [25] further extend this potential, reinforcing the synergy between AI-assisted programming, strategic reasoning, and interactive gameplay. Recent frameworks like CAMP [26, 21] with context-based RAG methods demonstrate how local-cloud architectures and retrieval mechanisms can further enhance the reliability and context-awareness of AI-assisted programming and software testing. These developments allow us to revisit Shannon’s question through a new programming paradigm of agentic engineering, where learned representations are programmatically leveraged–rather than relying on a full world model–to create hybrid, human-AI solutions.

A natural question arises: if modern LLMs can already play games such as tic-tac-toe or Nim when prompted directly, what is the value of a dedicated programming framework? The distinction lies in the difference between intelligence and programming. Prompting an LLM to play a game yields non-deterministic, often non-reproducible behavior that is opaque to the user and difficult to debug or improve systematically. Shannon’s taxonomy [2, 27] was developed when game-playing intelligence was largely deterministic and algorithmically grounded; however, current LLM-based systems are inherently non-deterministic. What is lacking is a structured programming paradigm that harnesses LLM capabilities within a reproducible, educational, and extensible framework. Nemobot addresses this gap by providing a programmable environment where LLM functions are treated as modular, inspectable components—analogous to subroutines—that users can design, test, refine, and share. Rather than merely orchestrating LLM API calls, Nemobot operationalizes Shannon’s taxonomy through programmable prompt engineering, crowdsourced strategy refinement, and a tiered curriculum that scaffolds learning from simple dictionary-based games to advanced reinforcement-learning agents. This positions the contribution not as a wrapper around LLM services, but as a research framework that investigates how classical game-playing paradigms can be systematically extended with LLMs for both AI research and education. Recent work in IEEE Transactions on Games further motivates this direction: Phillips et al. [28] demonstrated goal-oriented NPC interactions using LLMs, while Bassanelli et al. [29] highlighted the importance of principled gamification design for effective learning outcomes.

This paper revisits Shannon’s taxonomy of game-playing machines [2, 27] and extends it using modern LLM capabilities to introduce a programming paradigm for strategic game development. Shannon’s four categories, illustrated in Figure 2: dictionary-based machines with pre-stored solutions, mathematically rigorous systems for solvable games, heuristic-driven machines for complex scenarios and learning machines that adapt through experience remain the foundational elements of intelligent systems. LLMs enhance these categories by compressing state-action mappings for dictionary-based games, applying symbolic reasoning for rigorously solvable games, integrating minimax algorithms and crowd-sourced data for heuristic-based games, and employing reinforcement learning with human feedback for learning-based games. Central to this exploration is Nemobot, a game-playing chatbot and interactive programming environment that embodies these principles. Nemobot empowers users to design and deploy game-playing agents, extending Shannon’s taxonomy with LLMs. Through games like tic-tac-toe, Nim, and Mancala, it showcases LLM-driven advancements in AI game programming, integrating natural language understanding and generation.

In summary, the contributions of our work are as follows:

  • •

An AI game programming framework called Nemobot Games is proposed, integrating language models with Shannon’s foundational concepts of game-playing machines. This framework uses LLM-based programming to enable AI game bots that interact with humans and adapt strategies. Through LLM functions like reasoning, decision generation, and neuralized memoization, the bots efficiently store and refine strategies, enabling trial-and-error learning with human feedback.

  • •

A systematic approach to collaborative prompt engineering through crowdsourcing is designed to enable programmable gameplay strategies and fostering creativity in AI game development with human feedback.

  • •

The Nemobot framework is evaluated in remote learning environments, highlighting its effectiveness in gamified AI education. By enabling collaborative prompt engineering through crowdsourcing, Nemobot fosters interactive learning and helps students grasp foundational AI concepts.

Section IV discusses collaborative prompt engineering, using real-world examples to align crowdsourced human feedback with strategic games and presents trial results in a practical setting. Finally, Section V explores open issues and future work, while Section VI summarizes the key insights and contributions of this paper.

II-A Shannon’s Taxonomy of AI Games with LLMs

For dictionary-based machines, Shannon described systems that predefine every possible game situation and store corresponding moves in a “dictionary” or function table [2]. Traditional implementations require large memory resources, limiting their practicality to simple games like tic-tac-toe. Building on this foundational concept, we propose that language models can be utilized to augment this approach by learning patterns from game data. We hypothesize that this effectively compresses the dictionary into a smaller, generalized model, allowing the system to infer moves for previously unseen positions without exhaustive storage. This approach provides new programmers a practical way to learn AI game programming by editing predefined moves in real time, gaining hands-on experience with LLM functions, deciding when to use the LLM or local memory, and understanding strategic decision-making in interactive environments.

In the category of machines using rigorous mathematical formulas, Shannon outlined systems capable of perfect gameplay in mathematically solvable games like Nim [27]. In the proposed framework, LLMs act as interactive copilots designed to explain the reasoning behind optimal moves in natural language. This design intends to make these systems valuable to the programmer, who benefits from the model’s ability to articulate the logic behind optimal moves in games like Nim. By integrating LLMs, these games can do more than just provide solutions; they can offer real-time programming assistance by breaking down complex calculations into step-by-step insights [30]. This bridges the gap between algorithmic execution and strategy formulation, making mathematically-proven strategies in AI games more accessible. Additionally, the concept of AI-assisted programming [31, 32] and existing LLM code generation capabilities can be utilized to create code for the mathematical strategies behind these games.

To illustrate, in the 1969 The Game of Euclid [33], players take turns removing multiples of the smaller pile from the larger, aiming to reduce one pile to zero. The game has an optimal play strategy tied to the computation of the Greatest Common Divisor (GCD). We demonstrate that LLMs can go beyond analyzing the game by generating code to implement the most efficient GCD algorithms, such as the classic Euclidean algorithm or optimized variants. For example, given two piles of aa and bb stones a>ba>b), an LLM can generate code to iteratively compute amodba\mod b, identify the optimal mm for reducing aa by m×bm\times b, and ensure efficient computation while avoiding losing configurations. The code generation capability of LLMs enables programmers to explore both the strategic elements of the game and the efficient implementation of mathematical algorithms, optimizing computational performance through context-aware code generation.

The third category, machines employing general principles of play, addresses games like chess, poker, and bridge, where no exact solutions exist but heuristic strategies guide decision-making [1]. Language models can synthesize extensive game-related knowledge, human strategies, and expert insights, enabling the generation of context-aware strategies for various scenarios. By incorporating techniques like Monte Carlo tree search and leveraging self-play, as demonstrated by AlphaGo [34, 35], LLMs refine strategies through training on both human and AI-generated games. Their chain-of-thought reasoning [36, 14] allows them to break down complex problems into step-by-step solutions, suggesting heuristics for optimal paths and recommending efficient search algorithms, thereby outsourcing computations like pathfinding and strategic exploration. This streamlines decision-making to enhance game performance with efficient algorithms, and fosters interaction between LLMs and human players who can test-drive strategies [32]. In programming these strategy games, LLMs can simulate skill levels and explain decisions through self-play, enhancing efficiency in probabilistic reasoning [35].

Finally, learning machines, the pinnacle of Shannon’s taxonomy, closely align with modern data-driven approaches. Shannon envisioned machines that enhance their strategies through trial-and-error learning, continuously refining their approaches based on extensive experience [1]. Similarly, LLMs can be integrated as adaptive tools to assist in the programming and refinement of learning through trial and error [37]. LLMs can guide the development of self-improving AI opponents by explaining feedback loops and training data’s role in learning. They accelerate reinforcement learning, particularly Michie’s Boxes algorithm in [38, 39], which is a stochastic, trial-and-error system that learns to avoid failures in unknown environments and is a direct intellectual ancestor of modern reinforcement learning [37]. By generating moves, evaluating outcomes, and refining strategies, LLMs act as AI coaches that simulate skilled opponents, provide gameplay feedback, and emulate reward models, while their natural language insights foster explainable strategic game play.

II Game Playing with Large Language Models

II-A Shannon’s Taxonomy of AI Games with LLMs

想象一张1950年的地图——那时候的计算机连「思考」是什么意思都不知道，但一位名叫克劳德·香农（Claude Shannon）的先驱已经为机器下棋画好了路线图。他把AI游戏系统分成三种「性格」：查表型、公式型和直觉型。更有趣的是，今天的大型语言模型（LLM）恰好能在这三条路线上都派上用场，而且方式截然不同。

查表型机器：一部厚重的攻略本

查表型就像把一本攻略本硬塞进电脑里——每一种可能的棋局都预先写好对应的走法，遇到什么局面就翻到哪一页。这种设计的好处是「绝对可靠」，缺点是体积太大：光是一个井字棋的所有变化就已经足够让内存吃不消，更别说是象棋或围棋了。

那LLM能做什么？它把整本攻略本「压缩」成了一个经验丰富的教练。与其存储亿万种可能局面，不如让模型学会「看到某种棋形，大概该怎么下」的规律。这样一来，没见过的局面也能推理出来，不必把所有答案都背下来。

这种设计对新手特别友好：你可以随时修改LLM的建议，观察它怎么回应；也能直观感受「什么时候该调用语言模型、什么时候用本地记忆就够了」。这就像学游泳时有个教练在旁——不仅告诉你怎么划水，还让你自己尝试、犯错、再调整。

公式型机器：会讲解题的数学老师

第二类系统走的是数学公式路线，专门对付有「标准答案」的游戏——比如经典的Nim游戏。理论上，这类游戏可以被完美解答，每一步都有最优解。

传统做法是让机器算出最优走法，但通常只给你一个答案，就像直接告诉你「这步棋应该下在E4」，却不说为什么。

LLM的加入让机器变成了「会讲题的数学老师」。它不只给出最优解，还能用自然语言解释背后的逻辑：为什么这步是最优的？用了什么数学技巧？这种设计让数学可证明的策略变得可理解、可学习——玩家不只是被执行策略的对象，而是真正理解自己在做什么。

具体例子是1969年的「欧几里得游戏」：两个石子堆，玩家轮流从较大的堆中取走较小堆的整数倍，目标是让对手无石可取。这个游戏的最优策略与最大公约数（GCD）的计算紧密相关。

更有意思的是，LLM不仅能分析这个游戏，还能生成代码来实现GCD算法——比如经典的欧几里得算法。假设两堆石子分别是 $a$ 和 $b$（且 $a > b$），LLM可以写出代码来迭代计算 $a \bmod b$、找出最优的移除倍数 $m$，确保高效计算且不落入输掉的局面。这种「从策略理解到代码实现」的一站式能力，让玩家既能学游戏策略，又能练编程功力。

直觉型机器：模仿人类高手的思考

第三类是最棘手的——没有标准答案的游戏，比如象棋、扑克、桥牌。这些游戏的复杂度使得穷举所有可能变得不可能，人类靠的是「经验」和「直觉」。

过去，程序员只能手工编写启发式规则告诉机器「大概该怎么下」；现在，LLM可以吸收海量的人类高手对局、策略评论、专家见解，合成出情境感知的策略建议。

更强大的是，LLM可以结合蒙特卡罗树搜索（MCTS）和自我对弈（self-play）技术——这正是AlphaGo击败李世石的关键。模型在人类对局和AI生成的对局中不断磨练，链式思考（chain-of-thought reasoning）能力让它能把复杂局面拆解成一步步推理，建议最优路径的启发式方法，甚至推荐高效的搜索算法。

这相当于把「计算」外包给LLM：让它做路径探索和策略推演，自己则专注于做决策。对开发者而言，这意味着可以用LLM模拟不同水平的对手，通过自我对弈来解释每步决策的依据，大幅提升策略研发的效率。

学习机器：从错误中进化的终极形态

你有没有想过，当机器也能「从错误中学习」时，它会变得多厉害？

这就是香农分类法中最顶层的——学习机器。它不像单纯计算机只会执行指令，也不像一般游戏机只会照剧本走，而是能透过trial-and-error（试误学习），一次又一次地从经验中调整自己的策略。这正是现代强化学习的核心精神。

现在想像一下：大型语言模型就像一个经验丰富的教练。它不仅能看懂棋盘或游戏局势，还能用自然语言告诉你「这一步为什么好」「刚才那一步为什么会输」。它能帮助AI对手自我进化——解释反馈回路是怎么运作的、训练资料在学习中扮演什么角色。

其中一个具体应用是加速 Michie的Boxes算法。这个算法的原理有点像「黑箱探险」：一开始不知道哪里有陷阱，只能乱闯；走错了会被电（失败信号），走对了继续前进。它是现代强化学习的直系祖先。而大型语言模型可以生成各种可能的走法、评估后果、然后帮忙优化策略——就像教练在旁边喊「这一步该这样下」。

这样一来，LLM不只是执行者，而是具备可解释性的策略顾问：它能模拟厉害的对手、提供游戏回馈、甚至取代传统的奖励模型功能。

Section 5 - II-B Programmable Prompts via LLM Functions

📄 原文

Large language models have the ability to synthesize knowledge from massive (raw and unlabelled) datasets, making them powerful tools for autonomous learning and decision-making [18]. We can design LLM agents that do not merely follow pre-programmed rules but can generate strategies, responses, and solutions based on the context they encounter. This approach aligns with Shannon’s vision of a machine that gradually writes more of its own orders. For example, in game-playing systems, instead of being limited to predefined strategies or rigid algorithms, a query can be made to different LLMs to generate feasible strategies to improve game learning performance iteratively with minimal human input.

Programmable prompt engineering further enhances this capacity by allowing developers to implement logical response behavior using LLM functions. Rather than writing exhaustive code to cover every possible situation, prompt engineering allows the program to dynamically understand and respond to varied situations through contextually relevant instructions in real-time. This allows for a flexible, scalable approach to problem-solving where the machine “writes” its own orders through prompt-based guidance. LLM functions thus serve to generate strategic decisions in games, adapt its behavior based on previous outcomes, and refine its strategies through feedback loops.

Hence, instead of relying solely on pre-programmed strategies, an AI game engine can autonomously generate new strategies by interpreting prompts and adjusting its play based on real-time feedback. This shift toward greater autonomy via machine learning for conversational data (see, e.g., [40]) is not only a recent technical achievement but also reflects a deeper philosophical transition in how we interact with machines, thus moving from instructing them directly to guiding them in a way that allows them to “write” their own programs via natural language generation and understanding. Michie’s contemporaneous “memo functions” or memoization in [41, 42, 37] are now understood as a form of speedup learning that stores and reuses previously-computed results, reducing redundancy and enhancing efficiency. Hence, LLM functions can be seen as a form of neuralized memoization, offering more dynamic, context-aware responses.

Indeed, Michie’s memo functions [41, 42] find a direct modern instantiation in the KV caching used by large language models, which stores previously computed attention keys and values to reduce generation complexity from quadratic to linear. Beyond this performance optimization, the concept can be expanded in three directions. First, semantic memoization would cache by embedding similarity rather than exact input match, allowing LLMs to retrieve responses for semantically equivalent queries without recomputation. Second, procedural memoization would cache not just results but successful strategies or tool-use sequences across agentic workflows, enabling agents to reuse effective plans. Third, distributed memoization would share cached knowledge across multiple agents, allowing collective speedup learning. Thus, treating computation as a learnable resource scales from symbolic caching to neuralized memoization and multi-agent learning, connecting to reinforcement learning by enabling adaptive learning where past interactions refine game strategies and improve collaborative problem-solving [37].

II-B 可程式化提示：讓語言模型自己寫策略

過去我們寫遊戲AI，就像請一個「完全聽話的工人」——你把所有動作规则写进程序里，它照着执行，一板一眼，不會自己思考。但如果我们给机器的不是死板的指令，而是一个「思考框架」呢？

這正是可程式化提示工程（Programmable Prompt Engineering）的核心概念。它允許開發者透過 LLM 函數（LLM Functions）來實作邏輯回應行為，簡單說，就是用「提示」而不是「程式碼」來告訴模型該怎麼做。過去要應對千變萬化的情境，程式設計師得寫一大串 if-else 條件把所有可能性都覆蓋——累死工程師，程式碼也變得難以維護。現在有了提示工程，模型可以根據上下文即時理解、回應各種情況，彷彿給了它一張「動態地圖」而不是一張「固定路線圖」。

這種做法其實呼應了資訊理論創始人夏農（Claude Shannon）早期的願景：機器應該能「逐步寫出更多自己的指令」。舉例來說，在遊戲對戰系統中，過去AI只能從預設策略庫裡挑選一個動作；現在工程師可以向不同的 LLM 發出請求，讓它們生成可行的策略，再根據實際對戰結果反覆調整，人類只需要在關鍵時刻點頭或糾正，其餘時間機器自己「練功」。

更有趣的是，這種「讓機器自己寫程式」的思路，其實在電腦科學裡有個老前輩——米奇（Donald Michie）提出的「memo 函數」（memo functions），也就是後來所說的記憶化（memoization）。什麼是記憶化？想像你算過「27 × 35 = 945」，下次再算同樣題目，你不會重新乘一遍，而是直接寫出之前算過的答案——省時又省力。LLM 函數正是這種思路的「神經網路版本」：不再只是記住答案，而是記住「成功的策略模式」。

具體來說，大語言模型中的 KV 快取（KV Caching）就是記憶化的具體實現——它把之前計算過的注意力鍵（keys）與值（values）存下來，下次遇到類似的輸入時，不需要重新算完所有注意力機制，複雜度從二次方（$O(n^2)$）直接降到線性（$O(n)$）。這就像遊戲裡的「快捷鍵」——上次打贏 BOSS 的招式組合，先記下來，下次遇到同樣敵人時直接叫出來用，省去重新思考的時間。

然而，作者認為這還只是起點。他們進一步描繪了三個未來方向：

  1. 語意記憶化（Semantic Memoization）：不再只比較「輸入是否完全相同」，而是用嵌入向量（embedding）的相似度來判斷是否可重用。也就是說，「怎麼打敗這隻怪獸？」和「如何擊倒這隻怪獸？」——就算用字不同，語意相近，快取系統應該懂得复用之前的好策略。

  2. 程序記憶化（Procedural Memoization）：不只存結果，還要存「成功策略的步驟」。這就像武俠小說裡的「武功秘笈」——不只記住最後打敗對手的那一劍，還要記得前面怎麼蓄力、怎麼調整呼吸，讓其他AI代理也能學這套「劍法」。

  3. 分散式記憶化（Distributed Memoization）：多個AI代理共享同一個「策略資料庫」。想像一間武術教室裡，所有徒弟都把練成的招式存進同一本秘笈，一個徒弟學會了，其他人也立刻能用——這就是多代理協作學習（multi-agent learning）的核心概念。

這三層擴展，把「計算」從一個被動消耗的資源，變成一個可以主動學習、累積、分享的「活知識」。作者甚至指出，這條路最終會通往強化學習——AI 透過過去的互動經驗不斷修正策略，在遊戲對戰或協作解題中變得越來越聰明。

Section 6 - II-C Collaborative Prompt Engineering

📄 原文

Effective prompt engineering is key to leveraging LLMs for specialized tasks like game-playing but often requires experimentation and expertise [43, 18]. Crowd-sourcing can potentially offer a scalable solution to harness collective intelligence to create and refine optimized LLM prompts for strategic gaming tasks [44, 45]. Indeed, the 1999 chess match between Garry Kasparov and “The Rest of the World” exemplifies large-scale crowdsourcing, where global participants combined human intuition, computer engines, and expert advice [46]. Kasparov’s narrow victory after 62 moves highlighted both the potential and limitations of collective decision-making in human-machine collaboration. Crowdsourcing offers a scalable way to improve natural language understanding by leveraging diverse participant perspectives to refine responses [40, 45]. Crowdsourcing will matter in agentic engineering by providing scalable human oversight and diverse training data that autonomous agents cannot generate on their own.

In strategic game programming, crowdsourcing enhances LLM fine-tuning by improving reinforcement learning models and simplifying evaluation. It generates prompts for strategy, move explanations, opponent simulations, and diverse gameplay styles, boosting AI adaptability. This collaborative process aggregates comprehensive datasets, uncovers high-performing patterns, and fosters innovation. By integrating these insights into LLM agents (e.g., Nemobot in Section III), it scales human creativity, enhances human-AI augmentation, and drives new programming paradigms in strategic AI game development, as illustrated in Figure 1.

The learning process in complex AI game playing will involve significant trial and error, but crowdsourcing can reduce the overhead by aggregating diverse feedback and human insights. Programmable prompts amplify crowdsourcing by providing a structured framework to encode, refine, and evaluate collective human expertise. III-C 互動式 AI 訓練

你有沒有想過，用像跟朋友聊戰術那樣自然的方式，就能「教」會一個 AI 下棋？這正是 Nemobot 設計的核心魔法。

什麼是 AI 的「訓練」？

任何 AI 系統說白了都在做三件事：看到環境（感知）、想一下該怎麼回應（處理）、然後動手（決策）。這套「看到→想到→做到」的流程，靠的就是所謂的「啟發式方法」（heuristic）——你可以把它想成 AI 的大腦裡那張「決策配方」。

這張配方有兩種做法：一種是直接把規則寫死（例如「如果對手只剩 3 顆棋子，你就一定要贏」），另一種是讓 AI 自己從對局中慢慢學會什麼時候該下哪步棋。Nemobot 厲害的地方在於，它把這兩條路都打通了，而且用一種非常親民的方式讓你參與。

像教小孩下棋那樣訓練 AI

想像你正在教一個初學者下棋：一開始你會說「盡量拿最後一顆」「不要讓對手湊成 3 的倍數」。這就是所謂的「初始啟發式」——用自然語言描述 AI 在各種情況下應該怎麼做。

Nemobot 的演算法就是這樣運作的：

H^{0} ← 初始啟發式，k ← 1，R ← ∅

簡單來說，這就是「下棋→檢討→改進→再下」的循環。每回合 AI 會根據當前的配方下幾步棋，你給它打分數（獎勵），它根據分數調整配方，直到它的表現讓你滿意為止。

同一套框架，兩種用法

這個訓練框架其實很彈性，端看你想要哪種「教學方式」：

  • 自動化機器學習：更新配方時，系統會自己用梯度下降之類的數學方法去找更好的策略，就像讓 AI 自己「頓悟」
  • 規則型 AI：你也可以手動修改配方中的規則，就像資深教練直接在指導棋譜
  • 懶人模式：如果你的配方已經很完美了，直接跳過訓練循環也能用

Nemobot 不挑食，全部支援。

從「想法」到「會下棋」：LLM 幫你翻譯

最貼心的是，你不需要寫程式碼。只要用自然語言或偽代碼描述你的策略，Nemobot 就會叫 LLM 幫你翻譯成 AI 能執行的指令。

舉個例子，在 Nim 遊戲（一種拿石頭的比賽）中，你可以這樣描述：

「AI 應該每次拿走的數量 = max(3 - 用戶上次拿的數量, 1)」

翻譯成代數就是：

n_{AI} = \max(r, 3 - n_{user})

其中 $r$ 是剩餘石頭數，$n_{user}$ 是對手上一步拿的數量。是不是很像在寫教案？

在遊戲場中驗證：Chat Playground

策略寫好了，接下來當然要實際對戰。Nemobot 提供了一個叫 Chat Playground 的互動介面，讓你在對話框裡直接跟 AI 較量。

流程是這樣的：

  1. 設定初始狀態 $s_0$
  2. 你下一步，AI 根據它的配方算出回應 $a_i$
  3. 系統記錄這局的獎勵 $R^k$
  4. 重複對戰，直到蒐集足夠數據

如果你懶得自己下，還可以啟動自動對戰模式——讓 AI 隨機乱下（模擬「群眾外包訓練」），或者讓它跟另一個 AI 互相練功。

根據表現調整配方

每輪對戰結束後，系統會秀出獎勵分數讓你分析。這時候你可以：

  • 叫 LLM 幫你想新的戰術
  • 直接修改規則
  • 把之前累積的訓練數據餵進去，讓 AI 學到更多上下文

配方可以很精確（像數學公式），也可以很抽象（像「優先發動攻擊」這種高階原則）。Nemobot 讓你有足夠的空間去實驗。

Section 10 - III-D Tiered Curriculum

📄 原文

Nemobot supports developers with varying expertise by offering structured pathways for crafting AI agents, inspired by Claude E. Shannon’s classifications of game-playing machines [2]: dictionary-type machines, complete analysis machines, approximate principle machines, and learning machines. These pathways guide developers from fundamental heuristic design to advanced learning-based AI implementations:

  • •

Foundational Development: Developers begin by constructing dictionary-type AI agents for predefined games, defining actions aia_{i} for each state sis_{i}. This provides insight into how decision-making heuristics influence gameplay outcomes.

  • •

Intermediate Rule-Based Systems: Using NodeJS templates, developers create custom games and build rule-based AI that follows decision algorithms f​(ai|si)f(a_{i}|s_{i}). Heuristics are iteratively refined based on testing data, introducing the principles of optimization.

  • •

Advanced Data-Driven AI: Developers employ LLM functions to design complex, adaptive AI systems. By leveraging automated testing methods and crowdsourced training data, they transition to building learning-based AI capable of self-improvement.

This structured progression focuses on heuristic-driven decision-making as the foundation and gradually incorporates data-driven methodologies. By simplifying initial development and emphasizing iterative enhancement, Nemobot ensures a smooth transition to advanced AI training techniques. Through agentic engineering where autonomous LLM agents plan, execute, and refine learning strategies, the system can dynamically adapt to individual learner trajectories, selecting appropriate tasks and feedback mechanisms without constant human oversight.

你有沒有想過，為什麼有些人學AI很快上手，有些人卻一直卡在某個階段？關鍵在於有沒有一條「階梯式」的學習路徑。Nemobot 設計了一套分層課程，讓開發者從最基礎的「字典型機器」開始，一步一步往上爬，最終能夠獨立打造會自我學習的 AI 系統。

這套靈感來自資訊理論大師 Claude Shannon 對「遊戲機器」的經典分類：字典型、完整分析型、近似原則型、與學習型。Nemobot 把這四種層次轉化為開發者可以實際操作的階梯，讓學習變得像爬樓梯一樣踏实。

第一層：從字典型 AI 開始

想象你第一次玩棋類遊戲，身邊只有一張小抄，告訴你「看到這種棋型，就走這一步」。這就是字典型 AI——開發者先為每個遊戲狀態 $s_i$ 預先定義好對應的動作 $a_i$。

這種做法雖然「笨」，但有兩個重要價值：第一，讓新手搞清楚「原來 AI 就是把人類的經驗轉換成規則」；第二，開發者會立刻發現——靠手寫規則太辛苦了，而且難以涵蓋所有變化。這時他們就會自然想要進入下一層。

第二層：加入規則與優化

有了第一層的基礎，開發者可以自己設計遊戲規則，並讓 AI 根據函數 $f(a_i|s_i)$ 來決定動作——這叫規則型 AI。這裡的關鍵轉變是：從「手寫所有動作」變成「寫一個 decision algorithm，讓 AI 自己推導動作」。

開發者會開始接觸到「優化」的概念：透過測試資料反覆調整規則，讓 AI 決策越來越好。這個階段就像學會了基本開車技術後，開始學習如何在山路、平原、高速公路上靈活切換策略。

第三層：打造會學習的 AI

最後，開發者使用 LLM 函數來建立學習型 AI——不只遵循既定規則，還能透過自動測試與群眾外包的訓練資料持續自我改進。

這裡的核心是 agentic engineering：讓 LLM 代理程式自己規劃、執行、與優化學習策略。也就是說，系統可以根據每個學習者的進度，動態推薦適合的任務與回饋機制，不需要老師一直在旁邊盯著。

為什麼這套分層設計有效？因為它呼應了人類學習的自然規律——先做小事、建立信心，然後逐步挑戰更複雜的任務。從字典型到規則型再到學習型，開發者不只是學會技術，更是體會到「為什麼要這樣設計」的思考脈絡。

Section 12 - IV-A Dictionary-Based Game: Tic Tac Toe

📄 原文

Tic Tac Toe is an ideal example of Shannon’s first category of game-playing machines, using dictionary-based systems that store precomputed solutions for all possible game states. With a manageable state space of 19,683 configurations, each state is paired with an optimal move. This deterministic approach ensures a well-defined optimal response for every game state, such as maximizing the chances of winning or forcing a draw. While effective, this method can be memory-intensive and complex to implement in rule-based AI games.

To address the limitations, we employ the LLM gaming agent, which replicates the behavior of a dictionary-based system without the need to explicitly store an extensive collection of state-action pairs or create complex nested conditional loops. The user interacts with Nemobot by making moves, and the LLM responds as the opposing player, maintaining the deterministic characteristics of a dictionary-based system. To facilitate interaction, the 3×33\times 3 game board is represented with cells labeled numerically from 0 to 88, corresponding to the positions on the board. Users input their moves by specifying the cell where they want to place their mark (e.g., “0” for the top-left corner or “44” for the center). To communicate with the LLM agent, the game state is serialized into a structured textual format that captures the positions of X, O, and empty cells. This structured input effectively describes the current state of the game in a manner that the agent can process and respond to. For instance, assuming the LLM agent is already aware of its role and the rules embedded within the configuration of Nemobot, the prompt for a given state could be written as below.

Example Prompt: Tic Tac Toe Strategy Current Configuration: Cell 0 (Top-Left) is occupied by ’X’. Cell 4 (Center) is occupied by ’O’. All remaining cells are empty. Objective: Analyze the board state and execute the optimal move for Player ’O’ to prevent a loss or secure a win.

Instead of precomputing all possible moves and storing them in a dictionary, the LLM gaming agent uses its pre-trained knowledge and contextual understanding to generate optimal moves dynamically. The backend sends this query to the LLM through an API, and the LLM responds with the most strategic move based on the current configuration. This dynamic querying process eliminates the need for exhaustive storage, effectively compressing the “dictionary” into the inference capabilities of the LLM gaming agent. For any given game state, Nemobot ensures that the responses of the agent are consistent and deterministic, adhering to the foundational principles of traditional dictionary-based machines. Although the LLM gaming agent dynamically generates responses, these responses align with established optimal strategies for Tic Tac Toe, with its decision-making grounded in its pretrained knowledge. This design ensures that the foundational knowledge required for selecting optimal moves is embedded into the LLM once, allowing it to infer the best moves even for previously unseen positions. This approach significantly reduces memory usage and computational overhead compared to traditional methods that rely on exhaustive searches or evaluating nested conditional loops for each decision. By leveraging the ability of the LLM gaming agent to generalize and interpret game rules, Nemobot could achieve enhanced scalability and adaptability, delivering efficient and accurate gameplay without the need for repeated rule-based evaluations.

井字棋的「終極攻略」：一個已經被算透的遊戲

你有沒有想過，為什麼井字棋永遠贏不了？因為這個看似簡單的 3×3 遊戲，早就被數學家「暴力破解」了。

什麼是「字典式」的遊戲系統？

想像你手上有一本「井字棋必勝秘笈」——無論對手怎麼下，秘笈裡都已經寫好最佳應對。這就是字典式系統的原理：把井字棋所有可能的棋局（共 19,683 種）全部預先算過一遍，每種棋局對應一個「最佳下一步」。對手走出任何一步，你翻開秘笈，照著答案抄——這就是為什麼井字棋無論怎麼下都只能逼和，因為「答案」早就寫死了。

這種方法的優點是確定性：保證每一步都是最優的，不會出錯。但代價也很明顯——你得像圖書館一樣把所有「答案」都存起來，遊戲越複雜，需要的記憶體就越驚人。

我們如何用 LLM 取代這本「秘笈」？

這裡有個巧妙的轉折：我們不需要真的去存那本龐大的秘笈，而是讓 LLM 扮演那本秘笈。

具體怎麼做？遊戲開始時，玩家在 3×3 的棋盤上填入 X 或 O。我們把棋盤編號為 0 到 8（例如 0 是左上角，4 是正中央）。玩家說「4」，表示要在中央下子。系統把當前棋盤狀態轉換成一段文字描述，告訴 LLM：「現在棋盤長這樣，輪到 O（也就是 AI）下了，請給出最佳一步。」

這時候，LLM 不需要去查一個巨大的資料庫——它靠的是自己訓練時學到的知識。就像一個經驗丰富的棋手，看到棋盤就知道該怎麼下。LLM 的「推理能力」取代了傳統字典的「儲存空間」。

為什麼這麼設計？

這裡回答一個關鍵問題：為什麼不直接用傳統字典就好？

因為記憶體和擴展性。傳統方法像是要把所有可能的棋局都背下來，遊戲規則稍微改變（比如變成 4×4），資料量就爆炸了。但 LLM 的方式是「理解規則」而不是「背答案」——它學會的是下棋的邏輯，所以可以靈活應對從未見過的棋局。這就像一個真正會下棋的人，不需要背所有棋譜，靠的是「理解」而非「記憶」。

不過我們保留了確定性：無論問幾次，同樣的棋盤狀態會得到同樣的最佳回應。這保證了 AI 的表現是穩定可靠的，不會「心情不好就亂下」。

這個性質有什麼意義？

這種設計大幅壓縮了儲存需求——傳統字典可能要存成千上萬筆狀態對應表，現在只需要一個會推理的模型。同時，因為 LLM 能「理解」規則，它比純粹的條件判斷更加靈活可擴展。這也是我們稱之為「壓縮的字典」的原因——用模型的推理能力，換取更小的記憶體佔用與更強的適應性。

Nim is a classic combinatorial game and an example of Shannon’s second category of game-playing machines, which use mathematical formulas. IV-A1 尼姆遊戲中的嚴謹數學公式

你有沒有想過，當一台機器下棋或玩遊戲時，它能否不只給出「下一步」，還能像老師一樣解釋「為什麼要這樣下」？

這節要說的，正是這樣的故事——一台懂得用數學公式算出最佳解，還能用人話把思考過程說給你聽的 AI。

從經典遊戲說起

尼姆遊戲（Nim）是個有百年歷史的組合遊戲，也是資訊論之父 Claude Shannon 所說的「第二類型」遊戲機器——也就是完全靠數學公式來決定每一步該怎麼走的系統。

遊戲規則很簡單：有一堆石子，雙方輪流拿，每次可以拿 1 到 3 顆。誰拿到最後一顆石子，誰就輸（這叫「最後拿的人輸」的變體版本）。

聽起來很直覺對吧？但裡面藏著一個精妙的數學規律。

藏在石子裡的數學秘密：Nim-sum

厲害的地方來了——數學家發現，只要算出所謂的 Nim-sum，就能立刻判斷當前局面是「穩贏」還是「穩輸」。

Nim-sum 的計算法則很特別：我們把每堆石子的數量用二進位表示，然後把所有位元「互相比較」，相同得 0、不同得 1。聽起來抽象，但實際上只要用一個簡單的 XOR（互斥或）運算就能一次搞定：

$$\text{Nim-sum} = a_1 \oplus a_2 \oplus \cdots \oplus a_n$$

其中 $a_i$ 代表第 $i$ 堆的石子數。

為什麼這個運算能告訴我們誰會贏？

這背後有個漂亮的數學道理：當 Nim-sum = 0 時，輪到你的局面是「必敗」的——無論怎麼拿，對手一定能在下一回合把 Nim-sum 再次變成 0，最終讓你被迫拿到最後一顆。相對地，如果 Nim-sum ≠ 0，你正站在「必勝」的位置上，總能找到一步把 Nim-sum 變成 0，把失敗的燙手山芋丟給對手。

這就像有個萬能公式：算出來是 0，就別掙扎了，盡量拖延；算出來不是 0，就一定能找到那一步「把局勢翻轉」的好棋。

Nemobot 怎麼玩？

在 Nemobot 系統裡，每個回合會發生幾件事：

  1. 把局面序列化：系統先把現在有幾顆石子、是誰的回合，寫成一段結構化的文字。
  2. 交給 LLM：這段文字會送進大型語言模型，讓它運用預訓練時學到的尼姆遊戲數學知識，找出最佳下一步。
  3. 翻譯成「人話」：關鍵來了——LLM 不只輸出「拿 2 顆」，還會同時附上解釋，讓使用者明白「為什麼要拿 2 顆」。

這就是 Nemobot 與一般遊戲 AI 最不一樣的地方：它不只會算，還會教。

具體長什麼樣？

舉個實際例子。如果場上還剩 8 顆石子，Nemobot 可能會收到這樣的提示：

Nim Game Logic
Game Status: A single pile remains containing 8 stones.
Constraints: You are permitted to remove 1, 2, or 3 stones.
Taking the final stone results in a loss.
Task: Apply the winning mathematical strategy (Nim-sum analysis) to calculate the precise number of stones to remove this turn. Provide a brief rationale for your decision.

這時 LLM 會算出 8 的二進位是 1000，Nim-sum = 8 ≠ 0，屬於必勝局面。它會進一步推導：只要拿走 2 顆，讓剩下 6 顆，6 的二進位是 0110，這時 Nim-sum = 8 ⊕ 6 = 2 ⊕ 6 = 0（變成必敗局面留給對手）。所以最佳策略是「拿 2 顆」——而且它會把這段推理用人听得懂的話寫出來。

為什麼這個設計重要？

這個設計解決了一個常見痛點：即使是正確的 AI 決策，使用者也常感到「霧煞煞」，不知道為什麼要這樣做。當 AI 能把 Nim-sum 的計算過程「翻譯」成自然語言，不僅提升了透明度，還讓這個數學遊戲變成一個教學工具——使用者可以在對戰中不知不覺學到離散數學與博弈論的核心概念。

Nemobot addresses these challenges by using LLM functions to dynamically query advice, enhancing game tree strategies without replacing them. IV-A2 採用通用原則的機器：Mancala 遊戲

說到需要「動腦筋」的遊戲，你可能會想到西洋棋或圍棋——但還有一種歷史同樣悠久的策略遊戲叫Mancala（非洲棋／播棋）。這正是電腦科學先驅克勞德·夏農（Claude Shannon）所說的第三類遊戲機器：它不靠蠻力列舉所有可能，而是依賴「通用原則」來下棋。

為什麼這種做法有必要？想像你要在網上下棋，如果每一步都讓電腦從頭推算所有未來變化——可能對手早就等得不耐煩了。Mancala 的決策空間極其龐大，預先算出「必勝招」根本不切實際，傳統做法是建一棵「遊戲樹」配合啟發式評估。但遊戲樹有個麻煩：它會隨著步數爆炸式成長，記憶體和運算成本嚇死人。

Nemobot 的做法聰明多了：它保留遊戲樹作為「決策骨幹」，但在關鍵時刻請出 LLM（大型語言模型）當即時顧問。這個 AI 不會替你下棋，而是根據當前局面分析：「現在有哪些策略可能比較好？」它從龐大的遊戲樹中動態撈出相關建議，大幅降低運算負擔。

遊戲是怎麼運作的？

第一步是「把棋盤變成文字」。系統會把當前雙方的棋子數量、雙方得分（雙方各有一個「Store」碗），以及輪到誰下棋，全部寫成一段結構化描述。這樣 LLM 才能「看懂」現在是什麼狀態。

具體來說，Mancala 棋盤是這樣編號的：

  • 底部那一排從左到右是 0 到 5（共6格）
  • 頂部那一排從右到左是 7 到 12（共6格）
  • 底部玩家的得分碗在 6，頂部玩家的得分碗在 13

玩家選格子時，只要說「我要走第 0 格」就可以了。

舉個具體例子：如果底部玩家在 pit 0 有 4 顆棋子、pit 1 有 1 顆、pit 2 有 3 顆，而頂部玩家在 pit 7 有 2 顆、pit 8 有 0 顆、pit 9 有 4 顆；此時底部玩家得分碗有 8 顆，頂部有 6 顆——系統就會把這整個局面寫成一段提示，請 LLM 幫忙分析「這一步該怎麼走最有優勢」。

LLM 怎麼幫忙？

遊戲樹已經預先算好了很多局面，但不可能涵蓋所有變化。這時 LLM 就派上用場了：它會分析當前棋盤配置，從遊戲樹中「撈」出最適合當前狀況的策略。

比方說，某個局面下底部玩家在 pit 0 有 3 顆、pit 1 有 2 顆、pit 2 有 4 顆，頂部玩家在 pit 7 有 1 顆、pit 8 有 0 顆、pit 9 有 5 顆。LLM 看完後可能會說：「走 pit 2 比較好，這步可以讓你再獲得一次額外回合。」這正是傳統 minimax 演算法很難做到的「靈活取價」——不是硬算到底，而是根據經驗法則給出好建議。

開發者可以透過這種方式，讓 LLM 從類似局面中「泛化」出不同的走法，而不是每次都從頭跑一次完整的遊戲樹搜尋。這就是 Nemobot 的核心創意：保留遊戲樹的严谨性，同時用 LLM 注入「直覺」與「彈性」。

The second prong involves crowdsourcing, where human players contribute diverse moves by playing against an agent using Shannon’s classical minimax algorithm [1]. This exposes the LLM agent to a broader range of tactics and strategies, improving its adaptability to different gameplay styles. Additionally, the LLM agent acts as an instructor, analyzing gameplay, offering real-time feedback on mistakes, and suggesting optimal strategies. This accelerates learning by highlighting suboptimal moves and guiding exploration toward better alternatives, fostering more strategic, long-term decision-making.

The leaderboard records player scores and provides valuable insights into the strategies employed by human players. This diverse input ensures that the LLM has access to a wide range of prompts, which can be integrated into its play during the LLM function programming, ultimately enhancing its adaptability and performance.

學習機器：用trial-and-error在遊戲中變聰明

還記得我們前面聊過的搜尋機器嗎？它像個勤快的算牌師，把所有可能的下法都算盡了才出手。但你有沒有想過：如果AI只能靠「算盡一切」來玩遊戲，那它永遠只能在人類給定的規則框框裡打轉？

現實世界可不一樣。我們人類學會一款新遊戲，通常不是因為有人把所有必勝策略寫進我們腦袋裡，而是因為我们自己玩過幾百場、慢慢摸清楚哪些步驟會輸、哪些步驟能贏。這個「從經驗中學習」的能力，正是接下來這群AI的厲害之處——學習機器（learning machines）。

從錯誤中變聰明：什麼是學習機器？

學習機器的核心精神很直白：不靠別人教，而是自己從經驗裡長出智慧。它會透過反覆嘗試（trial-and-error）、強化學習（reinforcement learning）等方式，一邊犯錯、一邊調整，最後變得越來越厲害。

這跟之前聊到的「搜尋的機器」形成了有趣的對比：搜尋機器靠的是「算盡一切」的蠻力，學習機器靠的則是「吃一塹長一智」的耐心。論文裡用了兩個具體遊戲——Nim 和 Mancala——來展示AI是如何「邊玩邊學」的。

Nim遊戲：讓AI自己在模擬器裡「摔坑」

先從 Nim 說起。規則很簡單：地上有幾堆石頭，兩人輪流拿，每次可以從任意一堆拿任意數量，拿到最後一顆石頭的人贏。

研究者做了一個網頁版模擬器，讓 AI 可以盡情「折騰」：

  • 任意設定初始石頭數量（N）
  • 任意設定每回合最多能拿幾顆（K）
  • 讓 AI 自己玩、自己錯、自己調整

這就像讓 AI 進入一個巨大的「遊戲練習場」——它不需要老師，只需要大量嘗試。贏了的招式，以後多用；輸了的招式，以後少用。

這些「練習紀錄」接著被拿來微調（fine-tune）大型語言模型：AI 學會的不是死規則，而是「什麼情況下該怎麼走」才能最大化贏面。遇到新局面時，它會參考過去的學習經驗，動態給出建議——這就是提示工程（prompt engineering）的妙用。

Mancala遊戲：雙管齊下的學習策略

Mancala（非洲棋）比 Nim 複雜得多。研究者在這裡用了兩條腿走路的策略，讓 AI 同時具備「自學」和「向人類學習」的能力。

第一條腿：自己跟自己对弈——Michie 的 Boxes 演算法

第一條腿叫做自我對弈（self-play），用的是 Donald Michie 在 1960 年代設計的經典方法，我們稱之為「Boxes 演算法」。

想像一下：遊戲的每個局面就像一個「盒子」，盒子裡放著好幾張「招式卡」，每張卡有不同的被選機率。當 AI 走出某一招並贏了，那張卡的機率就拉高；輸了，機率就降低。

就這樣反覆玩、反覆調，AI 慢慢從自己的失敗中學到什麼是好的策略——整個過程不需要任何人類老師示範，完全是「自己教自己」。這正是強化學習最迷人的地方：讓 AI 在試錯中自己長出智慧。

第二條腿：請人類幫忙「指點」——眾包數據

但光是自己練習，難免會陷入「自己以為對」的盲區。這時候，研究者找來了真人玩家幫忙——這叫做眾包（crowdsourcing）。

真人玩家跟 AI 對戰時，會使出各種「人類才有的直覺招法」。這些多元的策略被收集起來，成為 AI 的額外教材。

更有趣的是，AI 在這裡還扮演了教練的角色：它會分析人類的下法，即時指出「這一步不太好，應該走那裡」，幫助人類玩家更快進步，同時也讓自己學到更多元的策略。這種「以戰養戰」的方式，讓 AI 的適應力越來越強。

V-A Implications and Positioning in the Literature

To situate this work within the broader landscape of LLM-based game AI, it is instructive to compare Nemobot with several prominent systems. Voyager [48] is an open-ended embodied agent that uses GPT-4 to explore and acquire skills autonomously in Minecraft; however, it operates as a fully autonomous agent with no mechanism for human programmers to inspect, modify, or share the strategies it discovers. ChessGPT [8] bridges policy learning and language modeling for chess, yet it is specialized to a single domain and does not expose a programmable interface for educational use. Generative Agents [49] simulate believable human behavior through LLM-driven memory and reflection, but their focus is on social simulation rather than strategic game programming. Suspicion-Agent [10] integrates theory-of-mind reasoning with GPT-4 for imperfect-information games, demonstrating sophisticated multi-agent interactions but without an educational or programming-oriented framework.

In contrast, Nemobot occupies a distinct niche: it is a programmable, educational framework that exposes LLM functions as transparent, modular components within Shannon’s taxonomy. Users do not merely consume AI-generated gameplay; they actively design, test, and refine game-playing strategies through collaborative prompt engineering and crowdsourced feedback. This programmability distinguishes Nemobot from systems that treat LLMs as black-box players and aligns with the broader vision of AI-assisted programming [31, 26, 21] where human oversight and iterative refinement are central to the development process. By grounding its design in Shannon’s classical framework and extending it with modern LLM capabilities, Nemobot bridges foundational AI theory with contemporary practice, offering both research value and educational utility.

Finally, the algorithmic framework in this paper extends naturally beyond game playing to knowledge tracing and learning-to-learn in educational AI [50]. In knowledge tracing, a trial-and-error mechanism (e.g., Michie’s Boxes algorithm) can model a student’s evolving mastery of concepts: each knowledge component becomes a “box,” the student’s response (correct or incorrect) serves as reward or punishment, and the probability of selecting a particular strategy updates accordingly–enabling real-time prediction of future performance without requiring expert-labeled skill models. Crowdsourcing, meanwhile, aligns with learning-to-learn by aggregating solution strategies from diverse learners, allowing an intelligent tutoring system to meta-learn which pedagogical approaches work best for which student types. The trained AI agent acting as instructor within a classroom flipping model can become a meta-learning advisor that analyzes a student’s error patterns, generates personalized practice problems, and suggests alternative solution pathways [51]. This ensures that the complexity of tasks scales effectively with the learner’s proficiency. Together, these mechanisms can transform a strategic game-playing AI agent into an adaptive educational system that engages in learning-to-learn, continuously refining both its predictions and its strategic teaching approaches through interaction with an unknown environment, thereby acquiring meta-knowledge about how each individual learns over time.

說到這裡，你心裡可能有個疑問：市面上那麼多「用 AI 玩遊戲」的研究，Nemobot 到底特別在哪裡？

確實，近年以大型語言模型（LLM）為核心的遊戲 AI 如雨後春筍，但它們追求的目標很不一樣。我們用一張圖對比幾個具代表性的系統：

系統 核心特色 有無程式化介面

ChessGPT 專精於西洋棋的策略學習 無（單一領域）

簡單說，Nemobot 不是讓你「看 AI 玩遊戲」，而是讓你「和 AI 一起設計遊戲策略」。

為什麼「可程式化」這麼重要？

傳統的 LLM 遊戲系統像是開一輛自動駕駛的車——它怎麼開、你只能看，無法介入。但 Nemobot 把 AI 的決策過程「打開來」給你看：

  • 使用者能看到 AI 正在做什麼：決策邏輯是透明的模組
  • 使用者能修改 AI 的行為：透過「協作式提示工程」（collaborative prompt engineering）
  • 使用者能分享策略：就像程式碼可以被 fork 一樣，遊戲策略也能被社群改進

這呼應了 AI 輔助程式設計（AI-assisted programming）的願景——人類不是被動接受 AI 輸出，而是持續監督、反覆修正，最終得到更好的結果。

把訊農經典框架「現代化」是什麼意思？

1948 年，克勞德·訊農（Claude Shannon）提出了一套描述資訊與溝通的經典分類法，稱為「訊農 taxonomy」。Nemobot 把這套框架拿出來，重新填入現代 LLM 的能力——這不只是懷舊，而是讓研究與教學有一個共同的語言。

也就是說，學生或研究者可以用訊農的視角理解「AI 是如何處理資訊、做出決策的」，同時又能動手實踐——這就是 Nemobot 的研究價值與教育價值兼備的定位。

從遊戲到教育：一個更遠大的願景

讀到這裡，你可能會想：「好，這套框架玩遊戲很酷，但能用到其他地方嗎？」

答案是：不只可以，而且已經有明確的延伸方向。

第一個延伸是「知識追蹤」（knowledge tracing）。

傳統上，老師要判斷學生會不會某個觀念，需要專家先標註「什麼知識點會什麼時候被考」。但現實中，學生的學習軌跡充滿了「嘗試與錯誤」。Nemobot 的思路可以搬過來：把每個知識點想成一個「箱子」，學生的答對或答錯，就是給這個箱子的獎勵或懲罰。AI 根據這些回饋，動態調整「下次該讓學生練習什麼」——這就是不需要專家標註的即時學習表現預測。

第二個延伸是「學習如何學習」（learning-to-learn）。

想像一個課堂翻轉模型：老師不是直接教，而是讓 AI 當助教。這個 AI 收集來自不同學生的解題策略，歸納出「哪些教學方式對哪種學生最有效」。它甚至能分析學生的錯誤類型，生成個人化的練習題，建議不同的解題路徑——隨著學生能力提升，題目難度自動調整。

把這兩個機制結合起來，一個原本「玩策略遊戲的 AI」就變成了一個「會因材施教的 adaptve 教育系統」。它不只學會「怎麼玩」，還學會「怎麼教」——這就是所謂的 meta-learning（元學習）：學習「學習本身」。

Section 17 - V-B Open Issues and Future Directions

📄 原文

Future research will expand Nemobot to diverse AI tasks like multimedia generation and programming, enriching AI education. Inspired by Michie’s ideas on discovery as collaboration [42], future work could develop tools for real-time strategy refinement during gameplay, incorporating adaptive semi-supervised learning with fine-tuning to enable collaboration between three parties–a LLM-enabled copilot, human programmer and a Turing’s child machine in [42] (i.e., a local weaker LLM). Lastly, we list down several open issues remain in developing LLM-powered game-playing agents and collaborative programmable prompt engineering.

  • •

LLMs excel in short-term decision-making but face challenges with long-term strategies required for complex games like chess and Mancala, necessitating memory mechanisms or external models. Future research could explore hierarchical systems inspired by Shannon’s hierarchy [2], where learning advances through abstraction. At the highest level, programming copilots could leverage predicate logic for advanced planning, using inductive logic programming to derive predicates from examples and background knowledge [52].

  • •

Another area of exploration involves balancing generalization and specialization. While LLMs are trained on vast datasets to generalize effectively, applying them to specific game domains often requires extensive fine-tuning. Developing adaptive fine-tuning methods that allow for real-time adjustments based on gameplay feedback could enable LLMs to transition seamlessly between different game types or environments.

  • •

Additionally, the reliance on human feedback for fine-tuning poses scalability challenges. Although crowdsourced feedback can accelerate learning, ensuring the quality and consistency of this input remains a bottleneck. Future work could explore automated feedback systems or hybrid approaches combining human oversight with AI-generated evaluations.

  • •

Recent work on adversarial bots [53, 54] shows that, while capable of defeating top Go-playing AI (e.g., AlphaGo [9]), they are trained to exploit hidden vulnerabilities rather than develop well-rounded strategies. A challenge is thus to balance competitive performance with ethical design. Developers must ensure bots engage fairly and transparently, avoid exploiting human weaknesses, and foster challenging yet enjoyable gameplay. Achieving this balance with big data is crucial as AI systems advance, raising important ethical considerations for human-AI interactions in gaming [55].

  • •

Striking a balance between computational efficiency and reproducibility is a critical challenge, particularly for real-time interactive games that leverage big data. LLMs are highly resource-intensive, making their deployment at scale computationally expensive. To address this, research into lightweight, efficient LLM architectures and hybrid systems is crucial. These systems could offload less complex tasks to smaller, more efficient models while reserving the full capabilities of LLMs for tasks that demand greater sophistication. By achieving this balance, it becomes possible to not only enhance scalability and reduce costs but also ensure reproducibility by enabling consistent benchmarks for performance, resource utilization, and response accuracy across varied applications [55].

Addressing these challenges will not only improve AI game systems but also advance broader applications of LLMs in autonomous decision-making and interactive learning.

V-B 開放議題與未來方向

說到未來，你可能會好奇：Nemobot 之後，這條路會往哪裡走？

研究者描繪了幾條可能的方向。首先，擴展到更多元的 AI 任務——不只玩遊戲，還能幫忙做多媒體生成、寫程式，讓 AI 教育變得更豐富。更有趣的是，他們想到一種「三方協作」的可能性：除了人類程式設計師和 LLM 助手，還能加入一台「圖靈的孩子」——也就是效能較弱、但運算成本低的本地端 LLM。三個人一起合作，互相彌補彼此的強項，這個畫面有点像課業輔導時，老師、大學長和同學一起討論問題。

不過，在抵達那個願景之前，還有好幾個關卡要過。

第一關：長程策略的困境

你有沒有遇過那種「走一步算一步」的朋友？他們每一步都下得漂亮，但下了二十步之後，整個局面卻突然崩潰？LLM 就像這樣的高手——短期的決策能力很強，但面對需要「看十步後」的游戏如西洋棋或曼卡拉盤，LLM 往往力不從心。未來的研究可能會借鑲香農階層的概念，讓 AI 從抽象層次逐步學習；最高層的規劃甚至可能用到謂詞邏輯和歸納邏輯程式設計，讓機器自己從範例中學會「什麼是好的策略」。

第二關：通才與專才的拉鋸

LLM 之所以強大，是因為它讀過天文數字的資料，什麼都略懂；但要把牠變成某個特定游戏的專家，通常需要大量微調。這就像請一位通才教授去教小學數學——他雖然厲害，但還得重新準備教材。有沒有可能發展出「自適應微調」的方法，讓模型在玩遊戲的當下就能即時調整？這將讓 LLM 能夠在不同游戏類型之間無縫切換。

第三關：人類回饋的擴展瓶頸

現在的模型很依赖人類給的回饋來學習，但請人標註既貴又慢。雖然群眾外包能加速收集回饋，品質卻參差不齊。未來或許可以發展「自動化回饋系統」，讓 AI 自己評估自己的表現，或者用人類監督加上 AI 評估的混合方法，減輕人類的負擔。

第四關：對手太強，是好是壞？

最近有研究做出專門「找出弱點」的對手 AI，能打敗最強的圍棋程式 AlphaGo——但這些 AI 是被訓練去鑽漏洞，而不是真的變得更厲害。這就引發了一個倫理問題：我們到底要的是「會贏」的 AI，還是「會讓人覺得有趣」的 AI？開發者必須確保對手遵守公平原則，不會刻意利用人類的弱點，否則遊戲就不好玩了。這不只是技術問題，更是未來人機互動的重要課題。

第五關：效能與可重現性的拔河

最後也是最現實的問題：LLM 很貴、很耗資源。要在即時互動的遊戲中部署 LLM，成本很高。未來的研究方向包括開發更「輕量」的模型架構，或者採用混合系統——簡單的任務交給小模型處理，複雜的推理才動用大型 LLM。這樣既能省成本，又能保持結果的可重現性，讓不同研究之間能夠公平比較。

這些挑戰不只是遊戲 AI 的問題，它們也反映了 LLM 在自主決策、互動學習等更廣泛領域應用時會遇到的核心困難。克服這些關卡，或許能讓我們更接近真正「會思考」的 AI。

Section 18 - VI Conclusion

📄 原文

In conclusion, this paper introduces a new approach to strategic AI game programming, inspired by Shannon’s vision of self-programming automation and Minsky’s view of programming as a tool for refining ideas. Central to this is Nemobot, an interactive platform that allows users to create and deploy game agents. By integrating LLMs, Nemobot goes beyond static instructions, enabling AI agents to dynamically adapt to complex gameplay through inherent world models, crowd-sourced insights and iterative learning strategies.

This programming paradigm showcases how games in Nemobot surpass rigid algorithms, enabling flexibility and creativity in identifying patterns and evolving strategies in AI games. By autonomously refining their instructions and leveraging crowdsourced data to curate effective prompts, Nemobot can explore complex gameplay challenges for competitive and strategic play. Our approach aligns with Shannon’s vision of self-programming machines, where AI refines strategies through learning. Nemobot bridges AI theory with LLMs, offering educational value and new possibilities for dynamic game design.

結語

這篇論文提出了一套全新的策略性AI遊戲程式設計方法，它的靈感來自兩位大師的思想：香農（Claude Shannon）曾提出「自我編程自動化」的願景，認為機器應該能自己改進自己的程式；而明斯基則認為編程本身不是目的，而是幫助人類精煉想法的工具。我們把這兩個觀點結合起來，放進一個具體的系統裡——Nemobot。

Nemobot 是什麼？它是一個互動式平台，讓任何使用者都能創建和部署自己的遊戲AI代理。以往的遊戲AI通常是「寫死」的——設計師寫好一套邏輯，AI就永遠照著跑。但 Nemobot 不一樣：我們把大型語言模型（LLM）整合進來，讓AI不再只能執行靜態指令。它能透過「內在世界模型」理解遊戲規則，能參考「群眾來源」的玩家智慧，還能透過「迭代學習」不斷精進自己的策略。你可以把它想像成一個會自己讀攻略、會自己修改戰術的遊戲搭檔。

這套編程範式帶來的核心改變是：遊戲不再被僵化的算法綁死。AI能夠靈活應變、發揮創意，在複雜的對局中找出人類可能沒注意到的 pattern。我們讓AI自主精煉自己的指令，並利用群眾貢獻的數據來篩選哪些提示（prompt）最有效。這樣一來，即使是「不擅長寫程式」的一般玩家，也能透過提供遊戲洞察，間接幫助AI變得更強。

最後，我們想強調這件事的教育意義與未來潛力。Nemobot 不只是一個遊戲工具，它實際上把「AI如何自我學習」這件事變得可觸摸、可操作了。玩家在與AI互動的過程中，等於在旁觀察一個具體的「AI學習過程」。這與香農當年的願景一致——機器透過學習來精煉策略，而我們讓每個人都能參與這段旅程。從理論上來說，Nemobot 橋接了傳統AI理論與現代大型語言模型，為動態遊戲設計打開了一扇新的大門。
